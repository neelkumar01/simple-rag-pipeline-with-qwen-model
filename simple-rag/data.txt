Climate change refers to long-term changes in Earth's temperature, weather patterns, oceans, and ecosystems, and it has become one of the most significant environmental, economic, and social challenges of the modern era. Although Earth's climate has changed naturally throughout history due to factors such as volcanic activity, changes in solar radiation, and variations in the planet's orbit, the rapid warming observed since the Industrial Revolution is primarily associated with human activities. The large-scale burning of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas for electricity generation, transportation, manufacturing, and heating releases carbon dioxide, commonly written as CO2, into the atmosphere. Other greenhouse gases, including methane, nitrous oxide, and fluorinated gases, also contribute to global warming by trapping heat in the atmosphere through a process known as the greenhouse effect. Methane is released from sources such as livestock farming, rice cultivation, landfills, and oil and natural gas operations, while nitrous oxide is strongly linked to agricultural fertilizers and industrial activities. Deforestation further intensifies climate change because trees absorb carbon dioxide during photosynthesis, and the removal or burning of forests both reduces the Earth's ability to store carbon and can release previously stored carbon back into the atmosphere.

Global warming is an important part of climate change, but the two terms are not exactly identical. Global warming specifically refers to the increase in Earth's average surface temperature, while climate change includes a wider range of long-term effects, such as changes in rainfall patterns, stronger heatwaves, melting glaciers, rising sea levels, ocean warming, ocean acidification, droughts, floods, and changes in the frequency or intensity of some extreme weather events. The world's oceans play a major role in the climate system because they absorb large amounts of excess heat. As ocean temperatures rise, seawater expands, contributing to sea-level rise. At the same time, glaciers and large ice sheets in Greenland and Antarctica are losing ice, adding more water to the oceans. Rising sea levels can increase coastal flooding, erosion, saltwater intrusion into freshwater supplies, and the risk faced by low-lying islands and coastal cities. Communities living near coastlines may be forced to invest in flood barriers, restore natural coastal ecosystems such as mangroves, modify buildings, or, in severe cases, relocate to safer areas.

Climate change also affects the water cycle. A warmer atmosphere can hold more water vapor, which can influence the intensity of rainfall and increase the risk of heavy precipitation in some regions. However, climate change does not mean that every place will become wetter. Some locations may experience longer dry periods, declining soil moisture, and more severe droughts. This imbalance can create situations in which one region faces destructive flooding while another struggles with water shortages. Changes in rainfall and temperature can affect rivers, reservoirs, groundwater supplies, and mountain snowpacks. In regions that depend on seasonal snow and glacier melt for freshwater, changes in the timing and quantity of melting can create serious challenges for drinking water, agriculture, and hydroelectric power generation.

Agriculture is particularly sensitive to climate conditions. Crops such as wheat, rice, maize, and soybeans require suitable temperature ranges, water availability, and growing seasons. Extreme heat can reduce crop yields, while irregular rainfall can make planting and harvesting more difficult. Floods may destroy fields, and droughts may reduce the amount of water available for irrigation. Climate change can also influence the spread of agricultural pests, weeds, and plant diseases. Farmers may respond by using drought-resistant crops, improving irrigation systems, changing planting dates, diversifying crops, adopting soil conservation practices, or using climate forecasting technologies. These strategies are examples of climate adaptation, which refers to adjusting human or natural systems to reduce damage from current or expected climate impacts.

Climate change can have major consequences for human health. Extreme heat can cause heat exhaustion, heatstroke, dehydration, and increased stress on the heart and respiratory system. Older adults, young children, outdoor workers, and people with existing health conditions may be particularly vulnerable during heatwaves. Warmer conditions can also influence the geographic range and seasonal activity of some disease-carrying insects, including mosquitoes. Floods may contaminate water supplies, damage sanitation systems, and increase exposure to waterborne diseases. Wildfire smoke can reduce air quality and worsen respiratory conditions such as asthma. Climate-related disasters may also have psychological impacts, including anxiety, depression, trauma, and stress, particularly among people who lose homes, livelihoods, family members, or a sense of security.

Natural ecosystems are also affected by a changing climate. Plants and animals are adapted to particular environmental conditions, and rapid shifts in temperature or rainfall can disrupt habitats, food chains, migration patterns, and breeding cycles. Some species may move toward cooler regions or higher elevations, while others may not be able to migrate or adapt quickly enough. Coral reefs are especially vulnerable to marine heatwaves. When ocean temperatures become unusually high, corals can experience bleaching, a condition in which they lose the algae that provide them with much of their energy. Repeated or prolonged bleaching events can lead to coral death. In addition, oceans absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, which changes seawater chemistry and contributes to ocean acidification. More acidic ocean conditions can create difficulties for some organisms that build shells or skeletons from calcium carbonate.

Climate change is closely connected with economics and social inequality. Wealthier countries, companies, and individuals often have more financial resources to build resilient infrastructure, purchase insurance, use cooling systems, recover from disasters, and invest in new technologies. Poorer communities may have fewer resources to prepare for or recover from floods, droughts, storms, crop failures, and heatwaves. In many developing regions, people depend directly on agriculture, fishing, forests, or local water systems for their livelihoods, making them particularly sensitive to environmental changes. The term climate justice is used to discuss the unequal causes and consequences of climate change. It highlights the fact that communities that have historically contributed relatively little to global greenhouse gas emissions may still face severe climate impacts.

Governments, researchers, companies, and international organizations have developed different approaches to address climate change. Climate mitigation focuses on reducing greenhouse gas emissions or increasing the removal of greenhouse gases from the atmosphere. Renewable energy technologies such as solar power, wind power, geothermal energy, and some forms of hydropower can generate electricity with lower direct carbon emissions than fossil fuel power plants. Electric vehicles may reduce dependence on petrol and diesel, particularly when the electricity used to charge them comes from low-carbon energy sources. Energy efficiency measures, including better building insulation, efficient appliances, improved industrial processes, and smart electricity systems, can reduce overall energy consumption. Public transportation, cycling infrastructure, and urban planning can also affect transport emissions.

Forests, wetlands, grasslands, soils, and oceans naturally store carbon and are often described as carbon sinks. Protecting and restoring ecosystems can therefore support climate mitigation while also benefiting biodiversity and water systems. Reforestation involves restoring forests in areas that were previously forested, while afforestation generally refers to establishing forests in areas that have not recently been covered by forest. However, planting trees is not a complete replacement for reducing fossil fuel emissions. The type of ecosystem, tree species, land ownership, water availability, wildfire risk, and long-term management all influence the effectiveness of forest-based climate strategies.

New technologies are also being explored. Carbon capture and storage, often abbreviated as CCS, is designed to capture carbon dioxide from industrial facilities or power plants before it enters the atmosphere and then transport and store it, often in underground geological formations. Direct air capture attempts to remove carbon dioxide directly from ambient air. Green hydrogen is produced using electricity from renewable or other low-carbon sources to split water into hydrogen and oxygen through electrolysis. Supporters believe hydrogen may help reduce emissions in sectors such as steel production, shipping, and some heavy industries, although cost, infrastructure, energy requirements, and efficiency remain important challenges.

International cooperation is a central part of global climate policy because greenhouse gases mix in the atmosphere and climate impacts can cross national borders. The Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015 under the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change, created a global framework in which countries submit climate action plans known as nationally determined contributions, or NDCs. Countries are expected to update their climate commitments over time. Climate negotiations also involve discussions about climate finance, technology transfer, adaptation support, and loss and damage. Climate finance refers to funding used to support emissions reduction and climate adaptation, particularly in developing countries. Loss and damage discussions focus on climate impacts that cannot be fully prevented through mitigation or adaptation, such as the permanent loss of land caused by sea-level rise or severe destruction from climate-related disasters.

Businesses are increasingly considering climate-related risks. Physical risks include damage caused by floods, extreme heat, droughts, storms, and wildfires. Transition risks are associated with changes in regulations, technology, consumer behavior, energy systems, and markets as economies shift toward lower-carbon models. A company that depends heavily on fossil fuels may face new carbon regulations or declining demand, while a renewable energy company may gain new market opportunities. Organizations may calculate their carbon footprint by measuring greenhouse gas emissions related to their operations and supply chains. Emissions are often discussed as Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 emissions. Scope 1 emissions are direct emissions from sources owned or controlled by an organization. Scope 2 emissions are associated with purchased electricity, steam, heating, or cooling. Scope 3 emissions include other indirect emissions across a company's value chain, such as those related to suppliers, transportation, business travel, product use, and waste.

Individual behavior can contribute to climate solutions, although government policy, infrastructure, and industrial systems also have major roles. People may reduce energy consumption, use efficient appliances, choose public transportation, walk or cycle when practical, reduce food waste, reuse products, and support environmentally responsible policies and businesses. Dietary choices may also affect emissions because different foods have different impacts related to land use, livestock, fertilizers, processing, transportation, and storage. However, climate solutions vary by region. A strategy suitable for a large European city may not be practical in a remote village in Asia or Africa. Effective climate policies therefore need to consider local economic conditions, geography, culture, infrastructure, and development priorities.

Climate science uses many sources of evidence, including weather station measurements, satellites, ocean buoys, ice cores, tree rings, and computer-based climate models. Weather and climate are different concepts. Weather describes short-term atmospheric conditions, such as today's temperature or rainfall, while climate refers to long-term patterns measured over extended periods. A single cold day does not disprove global warming, just as a single heatwave does not by itself prove the full theory of climate change. Scientists examine long-term trends and large datasets to understand how the climate system is changing. Climate models use mathematical equations to simulate interactions among the atmosphere, oceans, land, ice, and other components of the Earth system. Researchers can compare different future scenarios based on variables such as population, energy use, technology, economic growth, and greenhouse gas emissions.

Addressing climate change involves scientific, technological, political, financial, and social decisions. Mitigation can reduce the scale of future warming, while adaptation can help communities manage impacts that are already occurring or expected in the future. Neither approach is sufficient on its own. Reducing emissions, protecting ecosystems, improving infrastructure, developing clean technologies, strengthening disaster preparedness, supporting vulnerable populations, and improving international cooperation are all connected parts of the climate response. The choices made by governments, industries, communities, and individuals over the coming decades will influence energy systems, economic development, ecosystems, and living conditions for future generations. Climate change is therefore not only an environmental issue; it is also connected to food security, water availability, public health, migration, national security, urban development, technology, finance, biodiversity, and global inequality.
